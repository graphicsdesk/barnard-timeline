Barnard secures Columbia classroom space ahead of 2024 closure of Altschul Hall

By Emily Forgash

September 14, 2023

Frances Cohen / Staff Photographer

Preconstruction work began in Altschul Hall last month, marking the first steps toward the building’s transformation into the Roy and Diana Vagelos Science Center, slated to open in spring 2026.

In fall 2024, Altschul Hall will officially close, rendering its 46,500 square feet of programmable classroom space unavailable for two years, according to Barnard’s renovation timeline. Barnard has entered into space negotiations with Columbia for the use of Chandler Hall and Fairchild Hall and is exploring other options to accommodate courses.

Altschul Hall is currently 167,000 square feet and holds 51 labs—17 teaching labs and 34 research labs across five science departments—and 12 classrooms including computer labs. It also houses the Lehman Auditorium, which has a capacity of 227 people.

The construction is the latest in a series of major renovations on Barnard’s campus—including the ongoing construction of the Francine A. LeFrak Foundation Center for Well-Being in Barnard Hall and the construction of the Milstein Center for Teaching and Learning in 2018.

The LeFrak center will focus on mental, financial, and physical wellness by bringing physical fitness spaces back into the building and providing various educational opportunities, according to the Barnard website.

The LeFrak Center will have a soft opening just before summer 2024, in which certain support spaces and corridors in the Barnard Hall area might open, according to a Barnard spokesperson.

The full opening of the LeFrak Center in fall 2024 will coincide with the official closure of Altschul Hall, providing an outlet for some space constraints. The new Ethel S. LeFrak ’41 and Samuel J. LeFrak Theatre will have a capacity of approximately 225 people, enough to temporarily replace the Lehman auditorium.

Rendering of LeFrak Center Courtesy of Barnard College

However, most Barnard science departments will move across Broadway to Columbia’s campus during the Altschul renovations.

“The various science departments have come to agreements with the Columbia departments to share, particularly lab space,” Barnard President Laura Rosenbury said in an interview with Spectator. “We’re very thankful that the departments at Barnard and Columbia were able to work together to find a solution.”

Barnard initially looked to rent space from Columbia, but Barnard was unhappy with the original price. Barnard then decided to consider external spaces in downtown Manhattan, which are often expensive while also being further away from campus. Amy Hungerford, executive vice president of Arts and Sciences and dean of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, said that Barnard then again began space negotiations with Columbia last winter, which were finalized a few months later.

Hungerford called the space negotiation—which enabled the renovation of Chandler Hall and will allow Barnard to have classes and principal investigators close to Barnard—a “win-win.”

“Everyone was motivated to get this right,” Hungerford said in an interview with Spectator.

The money that Barnard is paying Columbia for use of its space—which includes teaching labs—will fund a much-needed facelift of Chandler Hall. Hungerford said Barnard departments will use the second floor of Chandler Hall following renovations to accommodate them.

“It’s just a great example of the collaboration between Barnard and Columbia, and how the faculty support one another,” Rosenbury said.

Chandler Hall has been in desperate need of renovations for over two decades due to a lack of usability. The fifth floor, which is currently not in use, alone is expected to cost over $10 million to renovate, according to Hungerford.

This problem is not unique to Chandler Hall; a lack of modern updates affects many Arts and Sciences buildings. Columbia conducted a science master plan survey from 2019 to 2021 of all science buildings in the Arts and Sciences with Perkins&Will—an architecture firm that helps organizations evaluate their use of space. According to Hungerford, the survey found that Arts and Sciences does have available physical space to host all of their labs and classes, but the space is old and not fitted for modern science, and the Arts and Sciences does not have enough money to renovate it immediately.

Part of the changes to Barnard’s space might necessitate extensions to Barnard’s school day so that it starts earlier or ends later to avoid too much overlap with Columbia’s schedule. According to a Barnard spokesperson, plans regarding scheduling have not been finalized. Hungerford said that Columbia’s class schedule may change slightly to use time more efficiently.

“I think during this transition there will be some feelings of fear or frustration. And so we hope to just communicate as clearly as we can, but also keep the eye on the prize,” Rosenbury said. “In three years, this new space will be transformational.”

The R&D Science Center will boast between 87,000 and 99,000 square feet of programmable classroom space—almost double that of Altschul Hall. This shift comes after a widespread effort to promote science at Barnard under former Barnard President Sian Beilock’s administration, which included the launch of the Barnard Year of Science beginning in 2021.

Rendering of R&D Science Center Courtesy of Barnard College

The new construction will include a renovation of the existing spaces, an expansion northward, and an interconnection into Milbank Hall, according to the Barnard website.

Barnard announced the R&D Science Center in March 2022, following a $55 million donation from Diana T. Vagelos, BC ’55, who has served on Barnard’s board of trustees since 2003, and Dr. P. Roy Vagelos, VP&S ’54. The donation—which is more than double the previous single largest in Barnard’s history—is one of many made by the Vagelos family, whose donations have helped facilitate the construction of the Diana Center, the computer science center, and Barnard’s successful financial aid matching program. Columbia’s medical school—the Vagelos College of Physicians and Surgeons—is also named after the family.
